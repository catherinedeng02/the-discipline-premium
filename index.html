The Discipline Premium

What 31 years of S&P 500 data reveal about the cost of emotional investing
RESEARCH  |  BEHAVIORAL FINANCE
May 24, 2026

This note quantifies the long-run cost of emotional decision-making in equity investing. Using 31 years of S&P 500 data, it compares two investors who follow an identical monthly investment plan but differ in one respect: one holds through market declines, while the other sells in panic and re-enters only after a recovery. The reactive investor ends the period with 34.6% less wealth, a shortfall that proves consistent across a range of assumptions and concentrates around major market crises.

SECTION 01

The Scenario

Two investors begin in January 1995 and contribute $1,000 to the S&P 500 every month for the next 31 years. The first investor follows this plan without interruption and never sells, an approach this note refers to as the Buy-and-Hold Benchmark. The second, the Reactive Investor, follows the same plan in calm markets but sells the entire holding to cash whenever the market falls far enough from its prior peak, returning only once a clear rebound is underway.

The chart below traces both portfolios month by month. The two paths are indistinguishable for years at a time, then separate sharply whenever the Reactive Investor sells near a market low and buys back at higher prices. Each of those moments is marked, and hovering over any point shows the value of both portfolios in that month.

Both investors put in exactly the same amount of money, $377,000 in total, and both were exposed to exactly the same market. The only variable separating them is how each one responded to fear, which makes the gap between the two lines a direct measure of the cost of that response.

The shortfalls measured within these windows are smaller than the 34.6% figure for the full period, and that difference is itself the point. A crisis window captures the immediate damage of selling, but the larger cost comes later, as the Reactive Investor sits in cash and misses the early, steepest part of each recovery. That foregone growth then compounds silently for years, which is why the full cost only becomes visible over decades rather than within any single crisis.

SECTION 03

Robustness

A single result is only as credible as the assumption behind it. The headline figure uses a 20% drawdown as the panic trigger, which is the conventional definition of a bear market, but that specific number should not carry the entire conclusion. To test this, the same simulation is run with the Reactive Investor panicking at 15% and at 25% as well.

Across every threshold the pattern holds, and the reactive investor finishes well behind the benchmark in each case. The relationship is also orderly: a lower, more sensitive trigger leads to more frequent selling and a larger shortfall, while a higher threshold reduces both. Because the conclusion survives this range of assumptions rather than depending on one chosen value, it can be read as a genuine finding rather than an artifact of the setup.

SECTION 04

Discussion

The market did not take this money. The investor's own reaction to the market did.

The Buy-and-Hold Benchmark is best understood as a benchmark rather than a portrait of a typical person, and the phrase buy-and-hold here means only that its invested shares are never sold. Its purpose is to be free of emotion, so that the entire gap between the two portfolios can be attributed to the one behavior that distinguishes them. A real investor need not be perfectly disciplined to benefit from this finding; they need only be aware of where the cost comes from.

Several deliberate simplifications shape these numbers, and stating them plainly is part of the analysis. The model uses the S&P 500 price index and excludes dividend reinvestment, so the true ending wealth of both investors would be higher than shown. Because dividends would lift both portfolios equally, the gap between them, which is the actual subject of this study, is unaffected. The model also excludes transaction costs and taxes, and it assumes cash earns no return, which is a conservative choice that if anything understates the cost of sitting out of the market. The panic rule itself is a simplified, mechanical stand-in for a messier human reality, chosen because a clear and testable rule is more honest than an arbitrary one.

What remains after these caveats is a straightforward conclusion. An investor who contributed the same money to the same market, and differed only in reacting to fear, gave up roughly a third of their final wealth. The cost was not charged by the market but by the decision to leave it, and that is the discipline premium this note set out to measure.
